### AI and Machine Learning in Employee Performance Evaluation
Employees are the engine behind any successful business. Productivity at
one level compounds across the rest of the organization. When someone
works more effectively, it creates leverage. That leverage grows. Soon
it becomes the difference between keeping pace and leaping ahead.

Artificial intelligence and machine learning offer a way to unlock that
leverage. Not by replacing people, but by revealing insights. These
tools help identify patterns, spotlight growth areas, and speed up
decisions. When implemented well, they change how companies think about
evaluation, development, and hiring.

### How Goldman Sachs Uses AI to Support Knowledge Work
Goldman Sachs launched an internal AI platform in 2024. It includes
tools for summarizing investment research, translating documents,
generating code, and creating charts. One standout is Translate AI.
Before it launched, teams had to send documents to human translation
vendors. Now, employees can drop a document into the platform and get
instant multilingual output. That time savings adds up.

Another tool is their GS AI Assistant. It allows junior analysts to
summarize complex research and even identify gaps in analysis. This is
not generic content generation. The system is trained on the firm's own
data, including historical deal memos and internal reports, ensuring
that outputs match company tone and context. More than 10,000 employees
use these tools. Executives plan to roll it out to all 46,000 staff
members by the end of the year.

Goldman did not just push tools. They created guardrails. Every AI
output is traceable. The system logs what data was used and how
employees interacted with it. That kind of audit trail is essential in a
regulated environment like finance. And it builds trust.

### How PT Pos Indonesia Improved Fairness in Evaluations
PT Pos Indonesia, the national postal service, faced a common issue.
Managers used subjective measures to evaluate performance. Employees
often questioned fairness. Promotion decisions took too long. Complaints
were common.

They built a machine learning model based on job logs, attendance data,
training records, and customer feedback scores. The system used
classification algorithms to categorize employees into performance
tiers. Managers could not override results without explanation.

They also automated monthly feedback reports. Employees received a
breakdown of their performance relative to peers and suggestions for
areas of improvement. Within six months, HR reported a 30 percent drop
in internal disputes over evaluation results. Surveys showed employees
had greater trust in the system.

### How BetterUp Uses AI to Guide Coaching at Scale
BetterUp provides coaching services to enterprise clients. In the past,
they relied on live sessions with certified coaches. But this model
didn't scale well in global, hybrid teams. So they built an AI layer
called BetterUp Grow.

The system uses behavioral data from workplace tools to identify
coaching needs. For example, if someone frequently cancels meetings or
shows reduced activity in collaborative platforms, Grow might suggest a
module on time management or stress. Each employee gets a tailored
coaching track, with short learning bursts, reflection prompts, and
progress feedback.

They also use natural language processing to scan anonymous feedback and
identify team-wide themes. If multiple people in a region mention
burnout or disconnect, HR leaders can address it early.

95 percent of users said they preferred the AI-guided experience over
traditional LMS courses. Many noted that the learning felt more relevant
and timely.

### How Vizzy Reinvented Hiring with Psychometric Profiles
Vizzy is not just scanning resumes. They're replacing them.

Candidates create interactive profiles instead of submitting static CVs.
These profiles include psychometric test results, recorded elevator
pitches, and even mood boards. The idea is to reflect the whole person,
not just keywords.

On the employer side, recruiters can filter by desired traits. Looking
for someone with high conscientiousness and above-average abstract
reasoning? The platform shows matching candidates and highlights
potential areas of risk. It also includes anonymized peer comparisons
based on past hiring success.

Big brands use Vizzy to reduce mis-hires. Louis Vuitton used it to staff
a regional sales team. Instead of filtering by years of experience, they
filtered by adaptability, communication style, and past customer
satisfaction metrics from previous jobs.

Recruiters who used the system reported a 40 percent reduction in
time-to-hire and better alignment between new hires and team culture.

### Key Takeaway
AI in performance and hiring is not theoretical. It is here. Companies
are using it to automate low-value tasks, reduce bias, improve
transparency, and move faster. The impact is real.

But success depends on architecture. You need high-quality data,
well-scoped models, transparent outputs, and clear guardrails. When done
right, the result is not just automation. It is better decisions, more
trust, and a stronger path to growth.
